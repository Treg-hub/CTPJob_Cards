Your Role in the System

As a manager, you are the quality gate for the job card system in your department. The system works well when the data going into it is accurate and complete. Your job is not just to review what is happening on the floor — it is to ensure that every job card created in your department meets the standard required to build a useful maintenance record over time.

A job card with vague information is almost useless for analysis and future AI-driven maintenance. A job card with precise, complete information becomes a permanent record that helps the business make better decisions.

📋

Your two core responsibilities: (1) Ensure operators create complete, accurate job cards when they report faults. (2) Ensure technicians close jobs with adequate notes describing what was done and why.

Understanding the Four User Types

Operator

Discovers a fault and creates the job card. Responsible for accurate, complete information at the point of entry.

Technician

Receives the job card, attends to the fault, and closes it with a completion note describing what was done.

Manager

Oversees all job cards in their department. Responsible for quality, escalation response, and pattern identification.

Admin

Manages system configuration, employee accounts, and geofence settings.

ℹ️

Operators and Technicians are distinct roles. An operator reports — a technician resolves. Ensuring operators understand the importance of complete entries is part of your responsibility as a manager.

What the System Gives You

Real-Time Dashboard

Your Manager Dashboard gives you a live view of:

  • All open, monitoring, and recently closed job cards in your department
  • Which technicians are currently on site and which are off site
  • Jobs that have escalated — and why
  • Jobs that have been open for too long without progress

Notification History

Every notification sent by the system is logged. You can see who was notified, when, at what level, and what action they took — or did not take.

Escalation Notifications

When a P4 or P5 job has not been accepted within the escalation window, you will receive a notification. When this happens:

  • A fault has been on the system for several minutes with no technician response
  • You need to act — find a technician, investigate the gap, or attend yourself
  • This should be the exception, not the norm

What Is Required From You

1

Daily Job Card Review

Review all job cards from your department from the previous day. Check whether all reported faults were captured, jobs were responded to promptly, and whether recurring faults on the same machine are emerging.

2

Enforce Job Card Quality — Operators

Operators are responsible for the quality of the job card at the point of creation. You must enforce the standard. Incomplete or vague entries must be sent back for correction.

3

Enforce Closure Note Quality — Technicians

A job closed without a note is an incomplete record. Every closure must describe what was done, parts used, root cause, and any follow-up recommendations.

4

Monitor Escalation Patterns

If jobs regularly escalate past 2 or 7 minutes, investigate why. Repeated non-response by a technician is visible in the log and must be addressed.

5

Identify Repeat Failures

If the same machine appears in job cards more than twice in 30 days for similar faults, investigate. Review what was done each time and whether the root cause was truly resolved.

6

Verify On-Site Status Is Accurate

After new hires or phone upgrades, confirm your team's permissions are correctly set and their on-site status in the app reflects their actual location. Incorrect status means missed notifications.

Job Card Quality Standard — Operators

Every job card created in your department must meet this standard:

FieldWhat "Good" Looks LikeWhat to Reject
Department Correct, specific department Blank, "Other", or wrong department
Area Named area within the plant Blank or vague ("production area")
Machine Named machine or asset ID "The conveyor", "a pump" — no identification
Part Specific component affected Blank, "unknown", or "various"
Description Clear fault — what happened, what was observed, error codes "Broken", "not working", "fault" with no detail
Priority Accurate reflection of production impact at time of fault Blanket P1 to avoid pressure, or blanket P5 to jump the queue
⚠️

Priority accuracy matters. A P5 means production is standing. If operators routinely mark jobs P5 when production is running, the escalation system loses credibility. If everything is P1, genuine issues get buried. Hold your team to the correct standard.

StatusMeaningWhat to Watch

Monitor Fault resolved, machine under observation Who is monitoring and for how long? Review any older than 3 days.
Closed Job complete and confirmed resolved Does it have an adequate closure note?

Checklists

Weekly Manager Tasks

  • Review all job cards created in the past 7 days in your department
  • Identify jobs with incomplete fields — follow up with the operator or technician
  • Review closure notes — are they adequate? Are parts and root cause recorded?
  • Review escalation log — patterns in who is not responding?
  • Identify any machine with more than 2 similar job cards in the past 30 days
  • Check Monitor-status jobs older than 3 days — still being actively watched?
  • Confirm your team's on-site status is functioning correctly

What a Good Closed Job Card Looks Like

  • Department is correct
  • Area is specific and identifiable
  • Machine is named (not described generically)
  • Part is identified
  • Description explains the fault clearly — observed symptoms, error codes
  • Priority accurately reflected production impact at time of fault
  • Closure note describes what was done and parts used
  • Root cause is noted
  • Any follow-up recommendations are recorded

The Bigger Picture: Why Your Role Matters

The quality of this system is entirely dependent on the quality of the data entered into it. The system can only tell you what it has been told.

In the near future, AI tools will be added to identify patterns, predict failures, and guide technicians through fault-finding. Those tools will be trained on the job card history being built right now. If that history is vague and inconsistent, the AI tools will be limited. If it is accurate and specific, the system will be able to tell you which machines are likely to fail next month, what parts to stock, and what interventions have historically worked best.

Your job today is to build that history by enforcing the standard. Every complete job card is a data point that makes the system more intelligent over time.
